MODULE 1: NETWORKING & DEVICE IDENTITY

OSI Model (The Digital Map) - Slide 1

  • Definition: A 7-layer framework explaining how data moves from physical wires to apps.
  • Full Form: Open Systems Interconnection Model.
  • Investigation Use: Helps locate where evidence lives (e.g., Physical cable vs. App message).
  • Location: Standardized architecture used by every internet-connected device.
  • Evidence: If a suspect denies sending a message, investigators look at Layer 7 (Application) logs.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

OSI Model (The Digital Map) - Slide 2

  • Layer 2 (Data Link): Identifies the physical device via the MAC Address.
  • Layer 3 (Network): Handles routing via the IP Address.
  • During Investigation: Used to troubleshoot if a network was bypassed or physically tampered with.
  • How to read: Think of it as a stack; Layer 1 is the floor, Layer 7 is the ceiling.
  • Analogy: Layer 1 is the road; Layer 3 is the address on the envelope; Layer 7 is the letter content.

IP Addresses (IPv4 vs. IPv6) - Slide 1

  • Definition: A unique numeric label assigned to each device on a computer network.
  • Full Form: Internet Protocol Address.
  • Investigation Use: Identifies the "Digital House" or connection point used for a crime.
  • Public IP: The identity of the entire home/office network visible to the internet.
  • Private IP: The internal identity used by devices behind a local router.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

IP Addresses (IPv4 vs. IPv6) - Slide 2

  • IPv4 Appearance: Four numbers separated by dots (e.g., 192.168.1.1).
  • IPv6 Appearance: Longer alphanumeric strings (e.g., 2001:0db8...).
  • Where it's found: In device "About" settings or logs of visited websites.
  • During Investigation: Retrieved from server logs of victims (e.g., a hacked email log).
  • Note: Multiple devices in one house often share a single Public IP.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

Static vs. Dynamic IPs - Slide 1

  • Static IP: An address that never changes, like a permanent home address.
  • Dynamic IP: A temporary address that changes every time a device connects.
  • Indian Context: Most Indian mobile users (Jio/Airtel) use Dynamic IPs to save costs.
  • Investigation Use: Explains why an IP found in a log might point to different users over time.
  • Lease Time: The duration for which an ISP "loans" an IP to a specific mobile.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

Static vs. Dynamic IPs - Slide 2

  • Where it's found: Controlled and assigned by the ISP’s central server.
  • During Investigation: Requires a "Timestamp" (date/time) to identify the user correctly.
  • How to read: ISP logs show "Allocation Time" and "Release Time" for each IP.
  • Common Error: Arresting a person based on an IP without checking the exact time of the crime.
  • Static Use: Usually found in corporate offices, banks, or police headquarters.

MAC Address (Hardware ID) - Slide 1

  • Definition: A permanent "fingerprint" assigned to the physical network hardware.
  • Full Form: Media Access Control Address.
  • Investigation Use: Proves that a *specific* laptop or phone was present on a network.
  • NIC: The Network Interface Card—the physical chip that holds this address.
  • Location: On a sticker under the device or in "Hardware Info" settings.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

MAC Address (Hardware ID) - Slide 2

  • How it looks: Six pairs of characters (e.g., 00:1A:2B:3C:4D:5E).
  • Manufacturer Clue: The first 3 pairs identify the brand (e.g., Apple, Samsung).
  • Investigation Trace: Found in the "DHCP Logs" of a seized Wi-Fi router.
  • Permanence: Unlike an IP, the MAC address generally does not change with location.
  • Evidence Value: Linking a crime-scene MAC address to a suspect's seized phone is strong proof.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

DHCP Logs (The Link) - Slide 1

  • Definition: A record book showing which IP was given to which MAC address.
  • Full Form: Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol.
  • Investigation Use: The "missing link" between an anonymous IP and a specific device.
  • Where it's found: Inside the memory of a Wi-Fi router or an ISP's database.
  • During Investigation: Seized from the router at a crime scene or home office.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

DHCP Logs (The Link) - Slide 2

  • How to read: A table showing: [Time] | [Assigned IP] | [MAC Address].
  • Scope: The range of IPs the router is allowed to distribute.
  • Evidence: Proves that "Suspect Device A" was connected to "Home Wi-Fi B" at the time of the crime.
  • ISP Level: ISPs provide "Radius Logs" which are the high-level version of DHCP logs.
  • Importance: Essential when multiple people share the same Wi-Fi connection.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

Subnetting & Gateway - Slide 1

  • Subnetting: Dividing a large network into smaller, manageable "neighborhoods."
  • Gateway: The "Exit Door" (Router) that connects a local network to the internet.
  • Why it's needed: Helps narrow down which floor or office wing a suspect was in.
  • Location: Found in the "Network Status" of a computer or mobile.
  • Common Gateway IP: Usually ends in .1 (e.g., 192.168.1.1).
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

Subnetting & Gateway - Slide 2

  • Subnet Mask: Usually looks like 255.255.255.0.
  • Investigation Use: Identifies the local router (Gateway) that must be seized for logs.
  • ISP Gateway: The tower or exchange point where a mobile user enters the internet.
  • Reading it: If the Gateway IP is known, you can find the physical router controlling the traffic.
  • Clue: Different subnets often separate "Guest Wi-Fi" from "Employee Wi-Fi."
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

IMEI vs. IMSI (Handset vs. SIM) - Slide 1

  • IMEI: The unique 15-digit ID of the physical phone (The Handset).
  • IMSI: The unique 15-digit ID of the SIM card (The Subscriber).
  • Full Forms: Int. Mobile Equipment Identity / Int. Mobile Subscriber Identity.
  • Why it's needed: To track a suspect even if they switch SIM cards (track IMEI).
  • Location: IMEI is on the box or dial *#06#. IMSI is inside the SIM chip.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

IMEI vs. IMSI (Handset vs. SIM) - Slide 2

  • During Investigation: Found in Call Detail Records (CDRs) provided by ISPs.
  • Indian Context: IMSI in India starts with 404 or 405.
  • Blacklisting: If a phone is stolen, the IMEI is blocked so no SIM will work in it.
  • Evidence: Linking an IMSI (Person) to an IMEI (Phone) proves who was holding the device.
  • Note: Dual-SIM phones have two different IMEI numbers.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

Cell Tower Triangulation - Slide 1

  • Definition: Estimating a location using signal strength from 3 or more towers.
  • Investigation Use: Finds a suspect’s physical location even if GPS is OFF.
  • Accuracy: Very high in cities (50-100m); lower in rural areas (1-3km).
  • How it works: Measures the distance between the phone and multiple towers.
  • Location: Handled by the Telecom Service Provider's (TSP) network.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

Cell Tower Triangulation - Slide 2

  • During Investigation: Request "Tower Dump" or "Cell Site Analysis" from Jio/Airtel.
  • Azimuth/Sector: Tells you which *side* of the tower the phone was connected to.
  • How to read: You get Latitude/Longitude coordinates (e.g., 20.93°N, 85.86°E).
  • Evidence: Proves a suspect was "Present" at the crime scene area.
  • Timing Advance (TA): A value showing the exact distance from the tower.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

WiFi SSID & BSSID - Slide 1

  • SSID: The name of the Wi-Fi network (e.g., "Airtel_Home").
  • BSSID: The physical MAC address of the specific router emitting that signal.
  • Full Forms: Service Set Identifier / Basic Service Set Identifier.
  • Investigation Use: A suspect can change the SSID, but the BSSID stays the same.
  • Location: SSID is visible on phones; BSSID is found on the router's label.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

WiFi SSID & BSSID - Slide 2

  • Traceability: Global databases (like Wigle.net) map BSSIDs to physical locations.
  • During Investigation: Check "Saved Networks" on a seized phone for list of BSSIDs.
  • Public Hotspots: BSSIDs can identify which specific railway station or cafe a user was in.
  • How to read: BSSID looks like a MAC address (00:A1:B2...).
  • Hidden SSID: Some routers hide the name, but the BSSID is always detectable by tools.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

VPN/Proxy Mechanics - Slide 1

  • Definition: Tools that hide a user's real IP by routing data through a middle server.
  • Full Form: Virtual Private Network.
  • Investigation Use: Criminals use this to appear as if they are in another country (USA/UK).
  • Encryption: VPNs scramble data so the ISP cannot see what the user is doing.
  • Where it's found: Installed as apps (NordVPN, ExpressVPN) or browser extensions.
Dhenkanal Police - Cyber Training

VPN/Proxy Mechanics - Slide 2

  • Red Flag: If a log shows a foreign IP (e.g., Netherlands) for a local suspect.
  • How to read: IP lookups will show "Data Center" or "VPN Provider" instead of "Home ISP."
  • Challenge: Many VPNs have "No-Logs" policies, making them hard to trace.
  • Investigation Goal: If a VPN is used, focus on seizing the device to find logged-in sessions.
  • Proxy: Similar to VPN but usually only hides the web browser's activity.
